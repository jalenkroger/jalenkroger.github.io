Title IX and The Evolution of Opportunity: The Growth of Women's Sports at Nebraska

By Jalen-Thomas Kroger | Published March 2026

Over the last two decades, the University of Nebraska-Lincoln's undergraduate population has seen female enrollment outpace male enrollment. Guided by the landmark legislation of Title IX, women's sports on campus have experienced significant evolution and growth, providing more opportunities for female athletes than ever before.

A Growing Female Student Body

Since 2004, the demographic landscape of UNL undergraduates has shifted. Female students formed a smaller portion of the student body 20 years ago, but their numbers have steadily grown to surpass their male counterparts in recent years.

But Athletics Tells a Different Story

Despite more women enrolled on campus, male athletes continue to occupy the lion's share of roster spots. When we look at the raw participation numbers, men have consistently maintained a higher participation than women over the past two decades.

The Power of Multi-Sport Athletes

When measuring athletic opportunities, universities track both total participation and the "unduplicated count"—which signifies the actual number of individual athletes. The gap between these numbers highlights the prevalence of multi-sport athletes.

Expanding Opportunities

A single cross-country runner might also compete in indoor track and outdoor track, acting as three "participants" but only one "unduplicated" athlete.

In women's sports, these overlapping seasons provide dedicated athletes with year-round competitive opportunities and help universities maximize athletic participation for women.

The Elephant in the Room: Football

Football is entirely unique in college athletics because of its massive roster size. A Division I FBS football team like Nebraska's can easily carry over 130 athletes, and there is no equivalent women's sport that comes close. This single sport skews the numbers drastically toward men.

What if we remove Football?

If we look at the raw participation numbers without the massive influence of football, the picture changes completely. Without football, women actually hold more total athletic opportunities at Nebraska than men.

The Track & Field Bump

Track & Field and Cross Country are common sports universities use to manage Title IX compliance. They offer large roster sizes that balance out massive men's football rosters. At Nebraska, Women's Track and Field combined participation accounts for a massive portion of the total women's opportunities.

The Unduplicated Reality (Without Football)

Because track & field counts inflate total participation, it's crucial to check the unduplicated numbers. Yet, even when filtering down to unique individual student-athletes, the story remains the same: women still possess more opportunities than men when football is removed from the equation.

The Beach Volleyball Addition

Introduced in 2015, beach (sand) volleyball is a perfect example of women's sports expanding. The sport provided an entirely new competitive season for athletes, with the majority of early participants coming directly from the indoor volleyball team, giving these women year-round championship opportunities.

How Do We Compare?

Looking at the raw, unadjusted 2023 total participation numbers side-by-side with peer institutions helps put Nebraska into perspective. Many of these schools boast significantly higher female participation totals.

An Apples-to-Apples Comparison

However, comparing raw totals is flawed. Some peer schools sponsor massive women's programs like Rowing (often 60+ athletes) or Field Hockey, which Nebraska does not offer. If we recalculate the totals using only the 20 sports that Nebraska actually sponsors, the gap narrows dramatically, showing a much tighter race across the board.

The Unique Athlete Reality for Peers

So what about the true, unduplicated headcounts? When looking directly at individual student-athletes on these campuses, the gap between men and women often swings back exactly to where it started, revealing the fundamental realities of large football rosters despite the continued growth of women's opportunities.

Looking Ahead: Flag Football in 2025

To continue addressing these massive gaps, universities are increasingly looking at emerging sports. Nebraska announced the addition of a Women's Flag Football team. A roster of 20-25 women will provide a significant, immediate boost to participation opportunities, aiming directly to counterbalance the intrinsic football effect.

Continuing the Momentum

As the conversation around collegiate athletics evolves, the growth of women's sports remains a key focus. While large sports like football will always skew total numbers, the addition of emerging sports and the strength of multi-sport athletes show a powerful, continued commitment to expanding female athletic opportunities.

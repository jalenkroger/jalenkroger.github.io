PARADOX

Inside the 25-year inversion of the University of Nebraska’s fiscal identity.

By Jalen-Thomas Kroger | Investigators

A

In 2024, the University of Nebraska reached a tipping point that went largely unremarked in official press releases. For the first time in the system’s 156-year history, it cost more to manage the university than it did to teach the students.

01. THE ACADEMIC HOLLOW-OUT

The Funding Flow (2014)

A university's core mission is teaching. For decades, the flow of operating funds heavily favored the instructional faculty who actually deliver that product.

The Hollow-Out (2024)

But the pipeline has shifted. Over the last decade, inflation-adjusted spending on NU faculty dropped by 10%. Full-time tenure-track positions declined by 8.5%.

The Adjunct Reality

To keep classes running amidst these cuts, the university relies increasingly on non-tenured adjuncts (+38.7% growth)—cheap labor paid per course with no benefits or long-term security.

The Administrative Swell

The savings from hollowing out the teaching staff didn't save the system money. Instead, the administration stream swelled by 54% in real dollars over the same period.

The Year 2000 Baseline

In 2000, the University of Nebraska President made roughly $250,000, while the average faculty member made $68,000.

The Inflation Expectation

If those salaries simply kept pace with general inflation (72% since 2000), the President would make $430,000 today, and faculty would make $118,000.

The 2024 Reality

Instead, Jeffery Gold's $1,062,573 salary marks a new era—shattering inflation expectations. Meanwhile, faculty salaries shrank against inflation, falling short at $108,712.

04. THE TUITION PARADOX

The Inflated Burden

UNL tuition hasn't just grown; it has outpaced the economy. Compared to the 72% rise in general inflation, student costs have surged by 200%.

The Fiscal Dead Zone

While students pay more, state aid falls below the 2000 dollar value. This gap—the shaded area below—represents the "Budget Paradox" students are asked to solve.

05. THE PEER COMPARISON

A System Out of Balance

Compared to its institutional peers, the University of Nebraska's administrative growth stands out. While precise accounting definitions vary by state, Nebraska consistently allocates a higher proportion of its budget to institutional administration than nearly all of its peers.

The Efficiency Gap

High administrative costs are often justified as "investment in student success." However, when comparing administrative spend to outcomes like graduation rates, a different picture emerges. While top-performing peers like Illinois (71%) and Indiana (81%) maintain high outcomes, Nebraska's 4-year graduation rate sits at 46%—well below the median for its spending level.
